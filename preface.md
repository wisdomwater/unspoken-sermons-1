# Preface

There are some Christian writers who primarily instruct the mind. There are others who stir the emotions. George MacDonald does something rarer. He awakens the heart to see God more clearly.

Born in Huntly, Scotland, in 1824, MacDonald was a Congregational minister, novelist, poet, and one of the great Christian thinkers of the nineteenth century. Although he served only briefly as a parish pastor, his influence spread far beyond any single congregation. Through his preaching, his stories, and his remarkable imagination, he became a spiritual guide to countless readers, including C. S. Lewis, G. K. Chesterton, Madeleine L'Engle, and many others who found in his writings a vision of God that was at once profoundly biblical and astonishingly beautiful.

The sermons gathered in *Unspoken Sermons* were first published in three series between 1867 and 1889. Many of them began as messages MacDonald preached from pulpits throughout England after leaving pastoral ministry. They were not composed as theological treatises or systematic doctrines, but as living meditations upon Scripture—carefully turning a single passage until its deepest spiritual meaning shone forth. Even on the printed page, they retain something of the spoken word. One has the feeling of sitting quietly while a wise father gently unfolds the heart of God.

The title *Unspoken Sermons* has intrigued readers for generations. Some were indeed preached before they were published, while others appear to have been written primarily for the printed page. Whatever their history, the title captures something essential about their character. These are not sermons driven by rhetoric or argument. They are conversations of the soul—truths spoken as much to the heart as to the ear. They invite reflection more than debate, contemplation more than conclusion.

MacDonald's great gift was his unwavering confidence in the goodness of God. Again and again he returns to the same glorious vision: God is truly our Father, and Jesus Christ is the perfect revelation of that Father's heart. Every page breathes this conviction. Divine judgment is never separated from divine love. Holiness is not the opposite of compassion but its fullest expression. God's consuming fire exists not to destroy His children, but to burn away everything that keeps them from becoming fully alive.

Because of this, MacDonald often challenged the assumptions of his own day—and continues to challenge ours. He refused to believe that fear could produce genuine righteousness. He would not separate justice from mercy or holiness from love. His deepest concern was never to defend a theological system, but to help people trust the Father whom Jesus came to reveal.

His writings are not always easy. Like the mountain streams of his native Scotland, they are clear but deep. Victorian sentences often wander through valleys and over hills before arriving at their destination. His arguments unfold patiently, inviting readers not merely to understand but to behold. Yet beneath the language lies a remarkable simplicity. Again and again, MacDonald asks the same question: *What is God truly like?* Every sermon is, in one way or another, an answer to that question.

This devotional adaptation seeks to preserve that answer while presenting it in contemporary language. The goal has not been to simplify MacDonald's theology, but to remove the distance created by nineteenth-century style so that modern readers may hear the same gentle voice that first stirred so many hearts. The reflections remain faithful to the movement of his thought while expressing it in language suited for prayerful meditation.

As you journey through these readings, do not hurry. MacDonald rarely rushes toward application. He first asks us to behold the Father. Only when we have seen Him more clearly does he invite us to respond. Read slowly. Pause often. Carry a single thought with you into the day. Let it become prayer before it becomes practice.

If these pages accomplish anything, may it be this: that you leave not thinking more highly of George MacDonald, but trusting more deeply in the Father revealed in Jesus Christ. For that was always MacDonald's desire. He would gladly disappear from view if, through his words, you found yourself looking instead into the face of the One who is infinitely better than you had imagined.

May the Father grant you the heart of a child, the courage to welcome His refining love, and the joy of discovering, day by day, what it means to become truly His son or daughter.
