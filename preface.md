## Preface

My introduction to George MacDonald came through C. S. Lewis. Long before I had read a single page of MacDonald himself, I encountered him as Lewis's wise guide in *The Great Divorce*. Lewis spoke of him with such affection and reverence that I could not help wondering who this Scottish preacher was whose influence seemed to echo so deeply through Lewis's imagination. That curiosity eventually led me to MacDonald's own writings, and I discovered that the guide Lewis met in his story had become a guide for countless readers in the real world as well.

George MacDonald (1824–1905) was born in Huntly, Aberdeenshire, Scotland. He was a Congregational minister, novelist, poet, and one of the great Christian storytellers of the nineteenth century. Although he served as a pastor early in his ministry, his preaching soon stirred controversy. MacDonald proclaimed a vision of God whose love was deeper than fear and whose judgments were always directed toward redemption rather than revenge. His conviction that God's love would never cease pursuing His children—what many today describe as a hopeful or universal restoration—placed him at odds with many within his denomination, eventually leading him away from settled pastoral ministry. Yet what closed one door opened another. Through novels, fairy tales, essays, sermons, and poetry, MacDonald reached a far larger congregation than any single church could have held.

His influence has been remarkable. C. S. Lewis called him his "master" and credited MacDonald with baptizing his imagination. J. R. R. Tolkien admired his mythic imagination, even where he disagreed with his theology. Madeleine L'Engle drew deeply from his vision of a universe radiant with the love of God. Countless pastors, writers, artists, and ordinary believers have found in MacDonald a voice that is at once intellectually searching, spiritually profound, and deeply pastoral.

Among his greatest theological works are the three volumes known collectively as *Unspoken Sermons*. Published between 1867 and 1889, these essays were never preached from a pulpit in the ordinary sense. They were written meditations—carefully reasoned, passionately devotional, and rich with imagination. Their pages return again and again to a handful of themes that MacDonald believed lay at the very heart of the gospel: the Fatherhood of God, the absolute goodness of His character, the freedom of loving obedience, the refining purpose of divine judgment, the transformation of the human will, and the calling of every person into union with Christ.

MacDonald does not write as a systematic theologian building airtight arguments. He writes more like a spiritual guide leading the reader up a mountain. At times the climb is steep. His Victorian prose can be winding, his sentences long, and his assumptions shaped by another century. Yet those willing to stay with him often discover vistas of God's love that remain as fresh today as when they were first written.

That conviction gave rise to this devotional adaptation.

The pages that follow are not intended to replace George MacDonald's original sermons. Nothing could. Rather, they are an invitation to walk beside him in contemporary language while preserving, as faithfully as possible, the heart of his message. Each reading seeks to distill the central insight of one portion of the *Unspoken Sermons* into a reflection suitable for prayer, meditation, and daily discipleship. My hope has never been to simplify MacDonald, but to make his voice more immediately accessible to modern readers who might otherwise never venture into these remarkable works.

If there is one truth that runs like a golden thread through every sermon, it is this: God is exactly like Jesus Christ. The Father is not reluctant to forgive, nor divided against Himself, nor satisfied with merely pardoning sinners. He desires to make His children truly alive, truly free, and truly like Himself. Everything—grace, judgment, suffering, joy, repentance, and hope—serves that single purpose.

It is my prayer that these reflections do what George MacDonald himself always sought to do: not merely leave you thinking differently about God, but trusting Him more deeply. May they awaken a greater love for the Father revealed in Christ, a deeper confidence in His unrelenting goodness, and a stronger desire to walk in the light until faith becomes sight.

Welcome to the journey.
